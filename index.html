Research project

Spatial model checking for medical image analysis

VoxLogicA is a line of research on declarative image analysis grounded in closure spaces, spatial logic, and model checking. Its central question is whether medically relevant image-analysis procedures can be expressed in concise, human-readable specifications and executed with sufficient efficiency, accuracy, and transparency to support research and, in the longer term, clinical practice.

Software repository UI demonstration DBLP bibliography

The project begins with the 2019 TACAS paper introducing VoxLogicA as a spatial model checker for declarative image analysis.

Recent work extends this direction toward symbolic and hybrid neuro-symbolic segmentation methods, with case studies on BraTS and BrainWeb and software/interface work connected to THE, STENDHAL, and FM4HD.

A parallel research strand studies usability and interface design for bringing spatial model checking closer to medical researchers and clinicians.

Overview

Research context

VoxLogicA sits between formal methods, qualitative spatial reasoning, medical image analysis, and human-computer interaction. The aim is not simply to produce a segmentation tool, but to study a symbolic paradigm in which image-analysis procedures remain explicit, inspectable, and reproducible.

The mathematical basis of the project comes from spatial logic interpreted over closure spaces, a generalisation of topology suitable for discrete spatial structures such as digital images and graphs. This provides a foundation for reasoning about neighbourhood, reachability, distance, and region growth.

On top of this foundation, VoxLogicA implements ImgQL, a language for executable image-analysis specifications. The emphasis throughout the project is on procedures that remain short, compositional, and close to the level at which domain experts reason about spatial constraints in images.

The medical-imaging case studies focus on brain MRI, including glioblastoma segmentation and tissue segmentation in healthy brain images. Validation reported in the publications uses public datasets such as BraTS 2017, 2019, and 2020, together with BrainWeb.

More recent work investigates hybrid symbolic-subsymbolic procedures in which learned components and logical procedures interact, as well as interface design intended to preserve formal rigor while reducing cognitive overhead for end users.

Method

Symbolic specifications over images

In the VoxLogicA approach, image-analysis procedures are written as spatial-logical specifications rather than assembled as opaque pipelines. The relevant claim is methodological: domain knowledge can be encoded as readable and executable structure.

ImgQL and model checking

ImgQL offers operators for intensity, distance, reachability, connectedness, similarity, and derived spatial predicates. These specifications are then evaluated by a spatial model checker implemented in VoxLogicA.

The implementation combines model-checking algorithms with established image-processing libraries and parallel execution, making it possible to work with 3D medical images while keeping the procedure explicitly represented.

let flair = intensity(flair) let hi = flair >. 0.95 let vi = flair >. 0.86 let hyper = flt(5.0, hi) let very = flt(2.0, vi) let lesion = touch(hyper, very)

Why the approach is academically interesting

First, it connects spatial logic and closure-space semantics to a concrete and demanding application domain. Second, it makes room for explainability and accountability in settings where pure sub-symbolic methods are often criticised for opacity.

The AI in Medicine work argues that symbolic specifications can remain concise and directly inspectable, while the hybrid line of work shows how learned components may be integrated without abandoning the advantages of explicit logical structure.

The interface work extends this by asking how such methods can be made usable for medical researchers and clinicians without severing the link between formal meaning and practical interaction.
